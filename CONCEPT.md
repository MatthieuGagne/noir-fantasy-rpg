# FINAL NOIRE
### *A cyberpunk noir horror JRPG in the style of Hideo Kojima*

> "In this city, everyone's got a license. The problem is nobody chose what they're licensed for."

---

## The Pitch

A turn-based JRPG set in a rain-soaked, perpetually-night cyberpunk metropolis where magic is real, technology is older than it should be, everyone is complicit, and something beneath the city has been listening to every confession ever made in the dark.

**Hardboiled Cthulhu.** Chinatown meets At the Mountains of Madness meets Neuromancer, directed by the man who made a game where you carry dead people across a collapsed America while Norman Reedus cries in the rain.

The investigation starts with a missing person. It ends with the realization that some truths are not meant to be found — and now it's too late.

---

## Core Tone

- The world is beautiful and doomed, and the doom is older than the world.
- Every character has a monologue inside them waiting to get out — and something waiting to get in.
- The femme fatale is the most competent person in the room and she already knows what's coming.
- The hero is always exactly one bad decision away from becoming the villain — or the abyss.
- The villain has already given a speech explaining why they're right, and they kind of are, and it doesn't matter anymore because they've seen what's down there.
- Horror in this game is never jump scares. It's the moment you understand what you're looking at.

---

## Philosophical Backbone

**Human spirit vs. bureaucracy and a cold, indifferent world.**

The world of Final Noire is a system — layered, self-perpetuating, ancient — that does not care about the people inside it and will use them if they let it. This force manifests at three scales simultaneously:

- **The entity** — vast, patient, alien. Doesn't hate you. Doesn't know you. Built a machine to extract what it needs from you and waited.
- **The Bureau** — institutional, mundane, banal. Nobody inside it thinks of themselves as evil. They inherited the system. The evil is structural, not personal.
- **The city itself** — an environment engineered to make you small, to make resistance feel absurd.

All three are expressions of the same thing. Against all three: people. Specific, broken, choosing people.

The game's thesis: **the power was always human. They just convinced you to give it away.**

Everything the Bureau teaches about magic — where it comes from, who controls it, what it costs — is a lie designed to make people surrender something that was never the system's to take. The entity needs humans to believe their power is external, borrowed, conditional. Because if they knew it was theirs, they might keep it.

The ending does not save the world. It reclaims the self. Which is harder, and matters more.

## The Four Lenses

### Film Noir
- Perpetual night / rain / neon
- Hard-boiled internal monologue narration over gameplay
- Moral corruption at every level of society
- Femme fatale who subverts or redefines the archetype
- The past is a wound that doesn't heal
- Mystery/detective structure driving the plot — but the mystery is not what you think it is

### Lovecraftian Horror
- The ancient thing beneath the city is not evil. It is simply **other** — it has a want, but that want is alien enough that it functions like indifference. It doesn't hate you. You're a resource.
- The entity is in the process of **manifesting** — crossing from the between-space into full physical presence. It needs human psychic power to complete the crossing. The city is how it collects.
- The city's wrong geometry is not incidental — it is the entity's body pressing through from the other side. The architecture is wrong because something vast is becoming present inside it.
- The Summoner *looked at something* and it looked back and now she can't stop seeing it
- Witnesses to the truth don't go mad all at once. They go mad slowly, rationally, one explanation at a time
- The horror is always more bureaucratic than expected: the ancient evil has paperwork. There are forms. Someone signed off on this.
- Knowledge is the real weapon and the real curse — the more you understand, the less you can unknow it
- The entity cannot be killed or expelled. The best possible outcome is to **stop the manifestation short** — strand it between registers, unable to fully cross. Not a victory. Not a defeat. A stalemate. Which is very noir.

### Cyberpunk — Shadowrun Model
Magic and technology coexist. Both are real. Both are controlled. Both are lies.

- **The city runs on two power grids**: the digital infrastructure of the megacorps, and the ley architecture that nobody talks about officially
- Cyberware, bioware, and neural implants sit alongside mages, Summoners, and street shamans — sometimes in the same body
- The License Bureau is a megacorp with regulatory authority: it controls who can legally use magic *and* who can legally carry cyberware above a certain grade
- Black markets exist for both: unlicensed enchantments and grey-market implants move through the same underground channels
- Hackers and mages sometimes can't tell each other apart in the deep grid — both are reaching into places that weren't designed for human minds
- **Magic is not what the Bureau says it is.** It is not drawn from ancient forces or ley lines. It is latent human psychic ability — internal, innate, always the practitioner's own. The entire official mythology of magic is a constructed lie. The entity needed people to believe their power was external and conditional, because people who know their power is theirs tend to keep it.
- **The ley architecture is not natural.** It is a constructed collection system — infrastructure built to receive and channel human psychic output toward the entity. The Bureau administers it. Most Bureau employees don't know what it's for.
- **Sigils** — presented as crystallized fragments of ancient power. Actually harvest nodes: resonance amplifiers that make psychic output easier to collect. They slot into both flesh and hardware. The Bureau classifies them as controlled substances to maintain distribution control. There are two kinds:
  - **Official Sigils** — Bureau-issued, registered, and serialized. They amplify the user's psychic output, but the gain is split: a portion flows back through the ley architecture to the entity. The user feels more powerful. They are also more efficiently farmed. The Bureau frames this as a licensing fee.
  - **Jailbroken Sigils** — grey-market, modified to sever the ley return channel. The full output stays with the user. Illegal to possess. The black market for them runs through the same underground as unlicensed Summoning. What you equip is a political act.
- The entity predates the city, the tech, and possibly the concept of either. It did not simply wait — it *built*, slowly, through human proxies, across generations. The city is the machine it made.
- **The entity exists in two registers simultaneously** — it has a physical presence beneath the Meridian *and* it is an information structure that the deep grid is resonant with. This is not two separate aspects; it is one thing that predates the human distinction between meatspace and cyberspace. Going deep enough into the Grid does not bring you *near* the entity — it brings you to a layer that *is* the entity. Two entrances to the same place. The wrong geometry of the city and the wrong behavior of the deep grid are the same phenomenon: its body, pressing through.

### Final Fantasy DNA (FF4 / FF5 / FF6 / FF7)
- Fixed party members with defined archetypes — their role IS their identity; each broken in a specific way (FF4 / FF6 ensemble weight)
- The signature **tonal shift**: starts as a personal detective story, ends as something that cannot be fully described

**Combat: Active Time Battle** — each character has a fixed command set.

### Hideo Kojima's Touch
- At least one character who is aware something is wrong with their reality — and has been for a long time
- A secret organization with a grandiose acronym
- The "big twist" reframes everything you thought you understood about the protagonist
- A walking/travel section that is mechanically minimal but emotionally devastating
- Real-world actor likenesses for key characters
- Post-credits scene that raises more questions than answers — and one of the questions is wrong in a way you won't understand until a second playthrough
- A villain whose name, when you finally learn it, hits like a gut punch because you've heard it before without knowing
- Death is thematically central, not just mechanically present

## The Central Mystery

The protagonist — a disgraced enforcer turned unlicensed private investigator — is hired to find a missing Summoner.

Summoners are rare, Bureau-monitored, and feared. When one goes missing, it means someone powerful wanted her gone. The investigation pulls the protagonist into:

- A black market for illegal licenses and grey-market cyberware (people trying to escape their Bureau-assigned class)
- A conspiracy within the Bureau's enforcement arm that the protagonist was once part of
- The missing Summoner didn't disappear. She *went*. Deliberately. Because she found something and couldn't look away.
- The city is built on something it shouldn't be — not just architecturally, but *ontologically*
- The License Bureau has known about what's beneath the city for decades. Its true purpose was never to control people. It was designed to *harvest* them — to meter psychic output, direct it through the ley architecture, and deliver it to the entity. Most Bureau employees don't know this. They just inherited the system.
- The entity is not trapped. It is not patient. It is *almost done*. The manifestation is in progress. The wrong geometry is the proof.

**Act 1:** Detective noir. Personal. A missing person case. The city feels wrong but you can't say why. Magic works the way everyone says it does — until the cracks start showing.  
**Midpoint:** The Summoner is found. What she's become is not what anyone expected. The villain's plan is revealed — and it's already working. The party breaks.  
**Act 2:** Each character descends into their own version of the horror. The truth about magic surfaces — not all at once, but in pieces, each one reframing what came before. Reunion is not triumphant — it's survival.  
**Act 3:** The city is the dungeon. The final revelation: magic was always human. The ley architecture is a pipeline. The Bureau is the pump. The entity is almost through. The entity makes an offer. The player decides what "winning" means — but winning does not mean the entity is destroyed. It means the manifestation stops short. It means people keep what was always theirs.

## Summoners

### What They Are
Summoners are people *selected* by displaced beings — exiles unmoored from wherever they came from, existing in the gap between registers. A future Summoner doesn't seek out their being; the being finds them first. Intrusions begin: a presence at the edge of vision, dreams with wrong geometry, a voice at a frequency nobody else can hear. The Bureau runs a dragnet to intercept these people before unauthorized contact is made.

### The License
A licensed Summoner is a monitored freelancer. They register their beings, report their summons, submit to audits. The Bureau doesn't control who they anchor. It controls when and how often. The leverage the Bureau holds is not over the Summoner — it is over the being. Revoke the license and the anchor breaks. The exile goes back to nowhere. That is why Summoners comply. Not for themselves. For the thing they are keeping alive.

The lie embedded in this system: Summoners believe their power comes from the relationship with their being — that they borrow something, channel something, are vessels for something external. This is false. The power is theirs. It always was. The being is real, the bond is real, but the energy is the Summoner's own psychic output. Every licensed summon is a metered withdrawal from that output, directed through the ley architecture, delivered to the entity as tithe. The Bureau doesn't tell Summoners this. Neither do most beings — because the beings themselves exist in a register adjacent to the entity's, and some arrangements were made long ago that the exiles don't discuss.

### The Black Market
Unlicensed summoning runs through the Meridian. Brokers facilitate first contact between unregistered Summoners and displaced beings — they don't summon themselves, they create conditions. The Meridian's wrong geometry makes the membrane thinner. The Bureau knows the brokers exist. Catching them is harder than it sounds because the beings don't register on any scan.

### Vesper
Vesper didn't run because she knew too much. She ran because something she trusted took her somewhere she couldn't come back from.

Her being was trying to show her the truth — that her power was always hers, that every licensed summon had been a tithe, that the Bureau was a machine and she was fuel. Following that thread meant going somewhere the entity noticed. It noticed. It moved in.

There is more than one presence in her now. The being is still there, underneath — still trying to reach her, still the thread Reid can pull. But the entity is using her as a staging ground: a body already saturated with between-space exposure, already partially opened. She is the proof of concept for the manifestation.

Every scene with Vesper is a question of which one is speaking.

Reid knew almost nothing about any of this when he helped her escape. He had a read on a stranger and acted on it. That's who he is.

## Immediate Questions to Develop

- [ ] How exactly does the License Bureau control both magic and tech — what does a "license" look like in this world? What does the bureaucratic interface of harvest actually look like day to day?
- [ ] What is the relationship between cyberware and magic — can they interfere with each other? Amplify each other? Can cyberware be used to suppress or redirect psychic output?
- [ ] Who built the Bureau — true believers who discovered the entity and chose to serve it, or something more banal: a system that accumulated purpose across generations until nobody inside it knew the original sin?
- [ ] Protagonist Reid's specific backstory / what crime got them stripped of their license? Did Reid have latent psychic ability that the Bureau was harvesting through his enforcer work — and does he know?
- [ ] Do the displaced beings know about the tithe arrangement? All of them, some of them, or only the ones who made older arrangements? What does Vesper's being know, and when did it decide to show her the truth?
- [ ] Who are the party members, what are their archetypes, and what does each breaking point look like at its worst? How does each one relate to the revelation that their power was always their own?
- [ ] What is the villain's name, ideology, and their relationship to the entity — are they a true believer, or just a bureaucrat who climbed far enough to see what the machine actually does?
- [ ] What is the city's name and its geography — the districts, the underworld, the geometry that doesn't add up?
- [ ] What tone does the music take — jazz noir? orchestral dread? ambient industrial? synthwave with something wrong underneath?
- [ ] What does the entity's offer actually say? What does it understand about humans, if anything?
